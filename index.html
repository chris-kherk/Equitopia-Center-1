Behind the Vertical – Solutions from A Classical Training Perspective by Bianca Rieskamp – Part One

Read Post

In the first video you see a horse ridden incorrectly, as we often see nowadays. Not a single foundation of classical riding theory is fulfilled.

The horse is under tension and moves in a hurry, the rider doesn't get to impulsion.

As a result, the diagonal two-beat trot is disturbed and the hindquarters do not follow the forehand action. The back is not yielded and, due to the lack of suppleness and the hurried gait, the horse does not walk in a springy manner, but the football can be heard loudly.

The horse walks with its nose behind the vertical the whole time. It doesn't dare to look for the rider's hand and put its nose forward.

"It is not the purpose of this treatise to give ways and means of preventing impure gait from occuring in the first place."

Only this much can be said;: the rider is always to blame.

Instead of waiting calmly until the horse has found a steady pace in its natural tempo, the rider will forcefully demand the utmost from the horse, even though it is not ready for it.

And impatience at work always take its revenge." (Colonel (ret.) S. v. Haugk, in Sankt Georg Almanach 1954, p. 95, 1954)

"The manner of forcing a horse into posture and balance through pain (screwing it together) is to be absolutely condemned, as perfect harmony between rider and horse can never be achieved in this way." (Richard Wätjen, The Art of Riding in Words and Pictures, p. 67)
